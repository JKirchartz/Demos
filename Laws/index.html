Laws that control Software

Conway's Law

organizations which design systems ... are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations

— Melvin E. Conway

The way you talk to each other instructs the way your software works, and the way it communicates with your client. "Organisations often produce web sites with a content and structure which mirrors the internal concerns of the organisation rather than the needs of the users of the site." ~ Nigel Bevan

Hofstadter's Law

It always takes longer than you expect, even when you take into account Hofstadter's Law.

— Douglas Hofstadter

Time estimates are just estimates, the real work is rarely exactly what you expect because realtity is messy.

Brooks's Law

adding manpower to a late software project makes it later

— Fred Brooks (Author of The Mythical Man-Month)

It takes time to become productive in a new project, helping people become productive makes the productive people unproductive. Communication takes time, and there's only so many tasks that can happen simultaneously without people stepping on eachothers toes.

Miller's Law

All discussions of incremental updates to Bugzilla will eventually trend towards proposals for large scale redesigns or feature additions or replacements for Bugzilla.

— Mike Beltzner

Dave Miller owned the Bugzilla product, probably saw this happen a lot. Iterating on software tends towards scope/feature creep, or complete and total redesign. Redesign will likely ignore previously fixed bugs, it's better to pay off techical debt; not all suggested features are valuable to all clients.
