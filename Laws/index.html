Laws that control Software

Conway's Law

organizations which design systems ... are constrained to produce designs which are copies of the communication structures of these organizations

— Melvin E. Conway

Any piece of software reflects the organizational structure that produced it "Organisations often produce web sites with a content and structure which mirrors the internal concerns of the organisation rather than the needs of the users of the site." ~ Nigel Bevan

Further Reading

  • Conway on Conway's Law
  • How Do Committees Invent?

Hofstadter's Law

It always takes longer than you expect, even when you take into account Hofstadter's Law.

— Douglas Hofstadter (Author of Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid)

Time estimates are just estimates, the real work is rarely exactly what you expect because realtity is messy.

Hick's Law

The time to make a decision is a function of the possible choices he or she has.

— William Edmund Hick

British Psychologist; Menu Design: Random lists take longer to read, as alphabetical lists lead to certain assumptions as to where to find a command

Further Reading

  • The Paradox of Choice - Why More Is Less, by Barry Schwartz (2004)

Brooks's Law

adding manpower to a late software project makes it later

— Fred Brooks (Author of The Mythical Man-Month)

It takes time to become productive in a new project, helping people become productive makes the productive people unproductive. Communication takes time, and there's only so many tasks that can happen simultaneously without people stepping on eachothers toes. Too many cooks spoils the soup.

Miller's Law

All discussions of incremental updates to Bugzilla will eventually trend towards proposals for large scale redesigns or feature additions or replacements for Bugzilla.

— Mike Beltzner

Dave Miller owned the Bugzilla product, probably saw this happen a lot. Iterating on software tends towards scope/feature creep, or complete and total redesign. Redesign will likely ignore previously fixed bugs, it's better to pay off techical debt; not all suggested features are valuable to all clients.

Sturgeon's Law

90% of everthing is crud.

— Theodore Sturgeon

Sci-Fi Author; Low quality products dominate every market. Critic's Corollary: 90% of people will criticize 90% of what they see regardless of their ability to distinguish crud from non-crud

Hanlon's Razor

Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity

— Robert J. Hanlon

The first time. " misunderstandings and neglect create more confusion in this world than trickery and malice." ~ Goethe, The Sorrows of Young Werther (1774)

SNAFU Principle

Communication is only possible between equals
WWII turn of phrase; honest communication is only possible in non-punishing situations

Ninety-Ninety Rule

"The first 90% of the code accounts for the first 90% of the development time. The remaining 10% of the code accounts for the other 90% of the development time."

— Tom Cargill of Bell Labs

Murphy's Law

“If there are two or more ways to do something, and one of those ways can result in a catastrophe, then someone will do it.”
correlarry: Finagle's Law: anything that can go wrong, will. ~ This shift in phrasing is an example of Murhphy's law working on Murphy's law.

Putt's Law

"Technology is dominated by two types of people: those who understand what they do not manage and those who manage what they do not understand

— Archibald Putt

Putt's Corollary: "Every technical hierarchy, in time, develops a competence inversion." with incompetence being "flushed out of the lower levels" of a technocratic hierarchy, ensuring that technically competent people remain directly in charge of the actual technology while those without technical competence move into management.

Gall's Law

"A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simple system that worked. A complex system designed from scratch never works and cannot be patched up to make it work."

— John Gall

From Gall's book `Systemantics: How Systems Really Work and How They Fail.` The World Wide Web & Blogosphere are successes of this gradual build-up to complexity. But be wary, Gall says a few pages before this that "A simple system may or may not work."

Greenspun's Tenth Rule

"Any sufficiently complicated C or Fortran program contains an ad-hoc, informally-specified, bug-ridden, slow implementation of half of Common Lisp."

— Philip Greenspun

I'm not a LISPer, but the story goes that LISP contains the functionality to write any complex program, and any program written in a language without LISP's functionality would have to implement some LISP methods. Greenspun didn't have 9 other laws preceding this, and later Robert Morris added the corollary "...including Common Lisp" because a lot of Common Lisp implementations relied on C.
